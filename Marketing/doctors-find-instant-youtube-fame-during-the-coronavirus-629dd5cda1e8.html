Doctors Find Instant YouTube Fame During the Coronavirus

Even Dr. Pimple Popper is tackling the coronavirus

Andrew Zaleski

Mar 30, 2020·7 min read

Dr. Franz Wiesbauer could not believe what he was seeing.

It was the middle of February, and the Diamond Princess cruise ship was idling in port in Japan. On board, the number of passengers infected with the novel coronavirus was steadily climbing: first 10, then 200, then more than 600 cases. And as Wiesbauer looked on from his home in Austria, a slinking thought grew ever more pronounced in the forefront of his mind.

“‘Shit,’ I thought. When I saw how fast that virus spread within that ship, it was kind of scary,” he recalls.

Within weeks the coronavirus spread from China and across Asia, Europe, and the United States. So Wiesbauer, a trained internist and epidemiologist, did what he thought any doctor with a YouTube channel should do: He made a video. And it blew up.

“I made a video about flattening the curve and became famous,” he says. “I get calls from media now on a daily basis. There was never a time when a layperson was so interested to learn the stuff we have to tell.”

That first coronavirus video racked up more than 300,000 views. Since then, Wiesbauer has been putting up multiple new videos every week. He uses cartoons and graphics to explain such topics as herd immunity and seasonality in five- to 10-minute clips. Each video is hosted on the Medmastery channel, which provides medical instruction for doctors, nurses, physician’s assistants, and nurse practitioners.

Before the coronavirus, Wiesbauer’s videos would rack up a few hundred views. But a video he uploaded earlier this month about symptoms of coronavirus was viewed more than 700,000 times.

As the world grapples with the new pandemic, the average person with no medical training frantically searches for answers on how to prepare and respond. Many turn to federal, state, and local officials and health departments.

But a surprising number are also turning to people like Wiesbauer, tuning into YouTube and devouring once obscure medical programming. Doctor YouTube is now, it appears, all the rage.

In a matter of weeks, MedCram’s subscriber count jumped by 200,000 subscribers.

Over in the U.K., Dr. Ed Hope, an emergency doctor who has already seen coronavirus patients, has kept a pandemic vlog since March 17. Meanwhile in New York City, popular YouTube doc and family medicine physician Mike Varshavski has spent his time debunking coronavirus misinformation and calling out the need for more testing kits for his more than 5 million subscribers.

Even some YouTube doctors who are not epidemiologists are being flooded by new followers eager to glean any insights possible about what the disease is, how it spreads, how we might stop it, and how to stay safe. That opens up a potential trapdoor for people seeking pandemic information on YouTube, which has a problem with misinformation.

To address that concern, YouTube recently enacted a set of policies meant to prioritize “authoritative sources” on the coronavirus. Search results for the coronavirus favor videos from established news sources, and the platform is automatically de-monetizing videos that touch on the pandemic. Still, the doctors who spoke to OneZero say they’re seeing views on their videos spiking.

In 2012, Dr. Roger Seheult and Kyle Allred, a physician assistant and Seheult’s former student, founded the YouTube channel MedCram. Their goal was purely educational — to make all the materials students like Allred needed for school available online. Over the years, MedCram produced the occasional one-off video on other public health matters, like the Zika virus in 2016.

But in January, as news about the new coronavirus spread, MedCram published its first video on YouTube about the coronavirus. The six-minute video explains the virus’s origins in Wuhan, the basic fundamentals of what a coronavirus is, how this new coronavirus jumped from animals to humans, and how it spreads through the air and on contaminated surfaces. It now has close to 900,000 views.

“That just exploded in terms of views,” says Seheult, a practicing pulmonologist in southern California. “It was obvious very quickly that this was going to be a highly rated topic.”

In a matter of weeks, MedCram’s subscriber count jumped by 200,000 subscribers, and now numbers over 670,000.

Seheult has posted more than 40 coronavirus videos to date, tracing the scale and spread of the pandemic while also touching on how the disease infects the body and possible treatments. Scientific studies and emerging research also figure prominently in many of MedCram’s videos. At the most basic level, Seheult is trying to make science accessible — translating the language of epidemiologists into comprehensible morsels.

“Whenever I’ve taken care of patients in hospitals, sometimes families are very upset. A lot of that is from fear and the unknown. Once you go through what’s happening, why it’s happening, and what we’re doing about it, they’re able to deal with it a lot better,” he says.

A similar approach is being employed by Dr. John Campbell, a retired nurse and an academic doctor in England who also taught other nurses for nearly three decades. He has more than half a million subscribers, and published his first video about the coronavirus on January 26. He’s published daily videos since then. (Fox News even had him on this month as a guest.)

“We’ve really been learning as we go along, and you get a lot of common questions, like about transmissibility in the air,” he says. “I’m trying to raise awareness and communicate information in a clear way that people can understand.”

Medmastery, in addition to its videos that share general knowledge about coronavirus, is also making some of its previously recorded videos available for free — specifically, its suite of four courses on how to operate mechanical ventilators. So far, 10,000 healthcare workers in 500 hospitals worldwide have signed up for these classes on mechanical ventilation.

Offering crash lessons for health care workers battling the coronavirus makes sense. But doctors like Wiesbauer who are suddenly also finding themselves anchoring real-time coronavirus coverage are struggling to figure out where their voices can fit into a broader public-health conversation — or whether their voices even should enter the fray.

Wiesbauer is transparent about his qualifications. “I haven’t published a single paper on coronavirus,” he tells me. “You want to talk about scientific results and convey them in a manner that’s understandable for people, but then you’re getting drawn into a public conversation, and I don’t feel like I belong there.”

“I’m trying to raise awareness and communicate information in a clear way that people can understand.”

And it’s not always clear whether YouTubers should advertise every development, such as potential advancements.

“If there is something that could be beneficial, we feel like we have a duty to say something about it,” says Seheult. “At the same time, if that thing is a limited resource, and we announce it over YouTube, then there’s a shortage of it. We’re learning that with hydroxychloroquine.”

In episode 34 of MedCram’s coronavirus coverage, Seheult discussed the possible efficacy of the malaria drug chloroquine in treating symptoms — more than a week before the president discussed the drug in one of his press conferences. After that press conference, demand for the drug shot up. Just last week, ProPublica reported that doctors in some states are now hoarding it.

Demand for coronavirus content on YouTube is so high that medical authorities who aren’t subject matter experts feel a responsibility — and an opportunity — to weigh in. Dr. Sandra Lee, better known as Dr. Pimple Popper, grapples with the conundrum of what to share, and how to do so responsibly, almost daily. She’s famous for her pimple-popping videos, content she says some people listen to as a way to quell their own anxiety. Yet as a doctor, she has access to medical information about the coronavirus before it finds its way into news reports. Should she create and share videos about a lack of personal protective equipment in hospitals? Or should she stick to what she normally does?

“I feel like my responsibility as a physician is to give people the information that I think is the most important for them to know, but then I also have another job as a social media person,” she says. “I’m personally leery about crossing that line.”

Lee’s concerns nod toward a scourge of YouTube videos peddling chronic misinformation. Falsehoods about the pandemic are nearly as easy to find as reliable narrators of the virus. And in a time of great uncertainty, that opens up the door for concerned citizens around the world to listen to virtually anything that’s delivered by what seems to be a credible source. Rutgers University food microbiologist Don Schaffner highlighted such an example of this last week, when he criticized a YouTube video that told viewers that merely touching groceries from the store might transmit coronavirus — and, therefore, that all fruits and vegetables should be washed with soap and water, something Schaffner said should never, in fact, be done.

As the pandemic spreads, and more cases mount, doctors say they can provide a service by sharing videos that enable people to do something right now about their anxiety or misgivings over the virus. Seheult, for instance, is going to start sharing more information about the steps people can take to improve their immune systems that won’t promote panic-hoarding. (He just posted a video on the importance of sleep.) Lee recently made a video where she used a pair of gloves and black paint to demonstrate to people how to thoroughly wash their hands so that no surface of the skin is missed.

And while Wiesbauer’s main focus is on getting as many as one million health care workers signed up for Medmastery’s free classes in mechanical ventilation, he says he plans to keep publishing other videos about the coronavirus.

“Everyone’s becoming an armchair epidemiologist,” Wiesbauer says. “It’s fascinating to see how we’re in a time where everyone’s engaging with this information at a very deep level. That’s unprecedented.”
